On Quantum Mechanics

June 15, 2021

In traditional mechanics you can intuit many of the answers; at the very least, you can sanity check your answer. If two objects collide, you can be sure they won't cout out of the other side with greater momentum than they came in.

But with quantum mechanics you simply don't have those tools, and you are navigating through a dark forest with the flashlight of mathematics as your only guide. To make matters worse, those mathematics are not always linear, they are not always 3 dimensional or less, and they aren't always real.

Take the Schrödinger equation for example.

Some interesting tidbits:

  • Sometimes, expirimentation is necessary. No one's intuition (mathematical or otherwise) could have forseen quantization and the property of collapsing wavefunctions. True quantum randomness and probabilities of collapsing wavefunctions is certainly mathematically sound, but it took experimentation to prove that.
  • Using imaginary numbers is a convenient way to express an amplitude and phase shift in a single number, which is why they are used to describe superpositions (I should re-learn why imaginary numbers are so useful for rotation).
  • We thought light was just a wave, then discovered it to be quantized to photons (E = nhf). We thought other particles like electrons were particles, but then discovered their wavelike properties (Schrödinger equation).
  • Purely mathematical concepts like dot products, cross products, and even addition and multiplication have interesting fundamental connections to the physical world. "And" and "or" play a role as well.

\(m^{e} \pmod{N}\) is the encrypted message

Public key is the combination of \(N\) and \(e\)
